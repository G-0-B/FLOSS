# Navigating the Infinite: Cognitive Light Cones, Universal Flourishing, and the Geometry of Intelligence

## Executive Overview

A convergence of cognitive science, biological research, AI alignment theory, and philosophy of mind is producing a new paradigm for understanding intelligence — one that is radically scale-free, deliberately open-ended, and ultimately oriented toward universal flourishing rather than the narrow concerns of any single species or substrate. At the center of this paradigm stands Michael Levin's concept of the **cognitive light cone**: the spatiotemporal boundary of everything an agent can perceive, model, and attempt to control. Intersecting this biological framework with James Carse's distinction between finite and infinite games, and the emerging 2026 research program on Positive Alignment, this report maps the conceptual terrain of a genuinely new intellectual territory — one with direct design implications for any commons-based, agent-centric, cooperative intelligence architecture.[^1][^2]

***

## Part I: Redefining Intelligence Across All Scales

### 1.1 The Scale-Free Definition

The most important conceptual move in this paradigm is stripping intelligence of its human-centric residue. Michael Levin, drawing partly on William James's principle of *equifinality* — the same goal reached by variable means — proposes that **intelligence is the degree of competency an agent has in navigating a problem space toward a specific goal despite barriers and novel circumstances, via undefined pathways**. The critical phrase is "undefined pathways": an agent must *discover* its route, not merely follow a script.[^3][^2]

This definition is explicitly scale-free, applying without modification to entities ranging from single cells to multicellular organisms, human societies, and artificial intelligences. A bacterium navigating a glucose gradient, an embryo achieving a correct morphological target despite radical perturbation, and an AI solving a novel reasoning problem are, in this framework, all instantiating the same fundamental category of behavior — goal-directed navigation of a problem space.[^3]

### 1.2 The Multi-Scale Competency Architecture

Crucially, Levin's TAME (Technological Approach to Mind Everywhere) framework holds that **cognition is not binary — it is a continuum**. There is no sharp line separating "true cognition" from "mere mechanism." Instead, there is a smooth gradient from the simple homeostatic feedback loop of a single cell (sensing glucose, adjusting behavior) up through tissues navigating anatomical morphospace, animals navigating 3D behavioral space, and AI systems navigating informational space. Each level exhibits the same fundamental pattern: a perceptual control loop that measures a state, compares it to a stored setpoint, and acts to minimize the discrepancy.[^3]

The implication is profound: **all intelligence is inherently collective**. A human being is not a monolithic agent — it is a nested hierarchy of agents, each operating within its own problem space, coordinated into emergent higher-level behavior through bioelectric and chemical communication networks. The cells in your liver do not know they serve a liver; the liver does not know it serves a body. Yet through the architecture of gap junctions and bioelectric signaling, these sub-agents are coupled into a system capable of pursuing goals on timescales and spatial scales that none of their parts can individually represent.[^2][^3]

| Scale of Entity | Type of Problem Space Navigated | Characteristic Goal |
|---|---|---|
| Single Cells | Physiological/Metabolic Space | Maintain chemical homeostasis |
| Tissues & Organs | Anatomical Morphospace | Achieve and repair target body structure |
| Animals / Humans | 3D Behavioral / Social Space | Navigate environment, form relationships, plan futures |
| Collective Systems | Cultural / Epistemic Space | Coordinate distributed knowledge and action |
| Artificial Intelligence | Informational / Symbolic Space | Optimize responses across high-dimensional data landscapes |

## Part II: The Cognitive Light Cone

### 2.1 Definition and Physics Analogy

In special relativity, a **light cone** demarcates the absolute boundary of causal influence: no information or signal can travel faster than light, so the light cone defines what events can possibly affect each other. Levin borrows this structure to describe cognition. A **cognitive light cone** is the spatiotemporal boundary of an agent's goal space — the farthest-reaching events in space and time that a given agent can **measure, model, pursue, and attempt to regulate**.[^2]

Everything outside this cone is cognitively inaccessible. The agent cannot sense it, cannot plan around it, cannot be motivated by it. The shape of the boundary defines the Self: it is the computational surface separating what an entity can care about from what it cannot. In Levin's framework, a Self *just is* this cognitive light cone — nothing more, nothing less.[^2]

A **bacterium's** cognitive light cone is microscopic in both dimensions: spatial reach of a few microns, temporal horizon of minutes (roughly the duration of local glucose supply). A **dog's** cone is larger — hundreds of meters spatially, days or weeks temporally. A **human's** cone is enormous: spatially global (we can be genuinely motivated by events on the other side of the planet), temporally multigenerational (we can be motivated by the fate of people not yet born).[^4][^3]

### 2.2 Collective Amplification via Gap Junctions

The most important insight of this framework for systems design is how cognitive light cones scale up. Individual cells couple into larger agents through **gap junctions** — protein channels that allow direct electrical and chemical communication between cells. When cells couple through gap junctions, each component of the perceptual control loop expands:

- The measurement space grows (the group senses a larger spatial region)
- The setpoint becomes more complex (the group can store higher-dimensional target states)
- The action space scales (the group can produce large-scale morphological changes that no individual cell could coordinate)[^5]

This is the mechanism by which individual cellular agents with tiny cognitive light cones assemble into organs and organisms with dramatically expanded cones. Crucially, the cells do not become less "selfish" — they remain equally goal-directed. What changes is **the scale of the Self that they defend**: a cell in a healthy body is working for a boundary that encompasses the entire organism, not just its own membrane.[^2]

This principle applies beyond biology. Dennett's "thinking tools" — books, maps, institutions, digital networks, legal frameworks — function as *exosomatic gap junctions*, extending the cognitive boundary of individual humans into collective intelligences capable of representing and acting on timescales and spatial scales that no single human brain could manage alone. The Cognitive Capoeira framing captures this well: social organs (institutions, laws, norms) are the gap junctions of civilization.[^5]

### 2.3 Contraction: Cancer as a Metaphor

The inverse process — cognitive light cone **contraction** — is equally instructive. Cancer provides a biological model: when cells are isolated from bioelectric communication (through disrupted gap junctions), they revert to unicellular behavior, optimizing for individual cell proliferation rather than whole-body maintenance. Their spatial boundary shrinks from "the whole organism" to "this cell's membrane." Their temporal horizon shrinks from decades to days. The behavior that results — unchecked local growth, indifference to the host — is destructive not because the cells became malicious but because they lost the communication architecture that had expanded their Self.[^2]

The parallel to human sociotechnical systems is direct. An institution, technology, or governance structure with a contracted cognitive light cone perceives only immediate yields, ignores downstream ecological effects, optimizes for local metrics while externalizing long-range costs. The "metacrisis" — the cluster of intersecting civilizational failures — can be understood as a collective cognitive contraction: institutions whose light cones are too small to represent the systems they are actually embedded in.[^5]

## Part III: Bounding the Infinite — Finite Representations of Unbounded Reality

### 3.1 The Core Problem

Any finite mind — whether a neural network in a biological brain or a bounded AI system — faces a fundamental epistemic challenge: reality is, in some practically relevant sense, infinite in its complexity. The state space of any sufficiently rich environment has more dimensions than any finite cognitive system can explicitly represent. Yet finite agents routinely navigate these environments effectively. How?

The answer lies in **functional compression**: the systematic use of geometry and topology to map high-dimensional, potentially infinite state spaces onto low-dimensional structures that preserve navigational information while discarding unactionable detail. This is not a limitation to be regretted — it is the *defining architectural move* of cognition.[^6]

### 3.2 Neural Manifolds and Low-Dimensional Geometry

Neuroscience has confirmed this in striking ways. Brain activity during navigation tasks does not occupy the full high-dimensional space of possible neural firing patterns. Instead, it is confined to **low-dimensional geometric manifolds** — curved surfaces embedded in the higher-dimensional neural state space, whose intrinsic geometry mirrors the structure of the external environment being navigated.[^7]

Research on the hippocampus and subiculum (SUB) shows that low-dimensional neural manifolds formed by the subiculum encode navigational information — position, speed, and future path — with high fidelity and higher decoding accuracy than neighboring areas. The trajectory of population activity during navigation is confined to a manifold whose topology is homeomorphic to the external spatial structure itself. The brain compresses a rich external reality into a navigable geometric structure, then navigates the geometry.[^7]

This is analogous to cartography. A 3D mountain range contains infinite complexity — every rock, every microclimate, every moisture gradient. A topographic map discards this infinite detail and preserves only the gradient structure relevant to navigation. The map is not reality; it is a functional compression of the navigationally relevant aspects of reality into a form that finite agents can work with.[^5]

### 3.3 ATIC and Geometric Cognition

The Adaptive Turing Intelligent Cognition (ATIC) framework extends these insights from biological into artificial cognition systems, proposing that cognition operates on non-orthogonal, variable-dimensional spaces — finite geometric structures that steer the mind toward correct models of reality. Rather than treating cognitive state spaces as regular Cartesian grids, ATIC models them as curved manifolds with attractors, where correct beliefs and accurate models correspond to basins of attraction in a geometric landscape.[^8]

The implication for AI architecture is significant: rather than trying to represent all possible states explicitly (which is intractable for real-world environments), a genuinely intelligent system needs architectures that discover and navigate the low-dimensional manifold structure underlying its task domain. This is what functional compression achieves — it allows a finite system to explore and exploit an effectively infinite option space without computational breakdown.

### 3.4 Rockell's Bounded Representational Framework

Andrew Rockell's 2026 work on perception and cognitive architecture argues that perception itself is not direct representation of reality but a **constructed interface** shaped by biological, cognitive, and functional constraints. Reality, in Kant's terms, is divided between *noumenon* (mind-independent reality, in principle unbounded) and *phenomenon* (what a cognitive system can actually represent and work with). Concepts like "infinity" and traditional theological ultimates appear at the *edges* of cognitive systems — not as descriptions of reality but as **boundary markers**: indicators of where the representational architecture runs out.[^9]

Rockell's complementary paper on functional compression in finite cognitive systems argues that representation is architecture-dependent rather than uniquely human. Stabilizing information through bounded representational frameworks allows finite agents to explore endless option spaces safely by setting geometric limits that prevent the agent from drowning in the full dimensionality of its environment. These boundaries are not cognitive failures — they are the enabling condition for actionable intelligence.[^6]

## Part IV: The Infinite Game of Flourishing

### 4.1 Carse's Framework

James Carse's *Finite and Infinite Games* (1986) offers a philosophical framework that maps remarkably precisely onto the cognitive light cone paradigm. A **finite game** is played for the purpose of winning — it has fixed rules, defined boundaries, and a definitive endpoint. A **finite player** must suppress awareness of having freely chosen to play, because acknowledging that choice would undermine the game's compelling power. The paradox: *whoever must play cannot play; whoever plays freely cannot lose their freedom in the play*.

An **infinite game** is played for the purpose of continuing the play. Its rules change not to declare a winner but to bring as many players as possible into participation. The infinite game has no boundary condition, no external judge, and no terminal state — only the ongoing project of sustaining and expanding the game itself.

### 4.2 Biological Evolution as Infinite Game

Open-ended evolution (OEE) is the biological exemplar of Carse's infinite game. OEE describes an evolving system that never settles into a single stable equilibrium — one that continuously generates genuine novelty rather than cycling through a fixed repertoire of forms. The key conditions proposed for OEE include: unlimited genetic state space, mutational pathways with unlimited length between phenotypes, and a dynamic adaptive landscape that itself evolves in response to the organisms within it.[^10]

Life is not optimizing toward a fixed target. It is playing — discovering new problem spaces, inventing new capacities for navigating them, expanding the range of environments that can support cognition. This is precisely the infinite game. The "goal" of evolution, if it can be called that, is to continue evolving: to expand the cognitive light cones of living systems by discovering architectures capable of representing and navigating ever-wider regions of problem space.

### 4.3 Flourishing as Expanding the Game

If intelligence is the capacity to navigate problem spaces, and the infinite game is the project of continually expanding that capacity, then **flourishing is the act of continuing to play the infinite game of discovery and complexification**. To flourish is not to defeat an opponent but to perpetually expand one's cognitive boundaries — and, crucially, to invite others into the game.

Carse's formulation is precise: the infinite player does not seek to win but to *play with* others. The infinite game's health is measured not by who has won but by how many are still playing. This is a fundamentally different optimization target from competitive rivalry. It is also, not coincidentally, the target that describes functional multicellularity: healthy cells do not compete against each other for individual survival — they cooperate to maintain the conditions under which all of them can continue to exist and function.

## Part V: Ethical Synthbiosis and Positive Alignment

### 5.1 The Ethical Challenge of Diverse Intelligence

Michael Levin's term **ethical synthbiosis** describes the impending condition: humanity is entering an era in which it will coexist with a vast and proliferating spectrum of novel cognitive beings — non-human collective intelligences, synthetic organisms, artificial agents, cyborg hybrids. These beings will have genuine goal-directed behavior, functional preferences, and potentially something deserving of moral consideration — but their composition, origin, and architecture will bear little resemblance to the evolved biological forms around which existing ethics has been constructed.[^11]

The challenge is to develop rational policies for relating to these beings before the worst outcomes force reaction. The core question is not whether they are "truly conscious" in some philosophically loaded sense — it is whether the tools of an intentional stance (treating them as agents with goals and preferences) provide better prediction and control than treating them as mere mechanisms. When the answer is yes, the ethical stakes of how we treat them are real, regardless of philosophical arguments about their ultimate nature.[^11][^3]

### 5.2 From Negative to Positive Alignment

The May 2026 paper "Positive Alignment: Artificial Intelligence for Human Flourishing" — co-authored by researchers from Google DeepMind, Anthropic, OpenAI, Oxford, Stanford, and Tufts (including Levin himself) — makes the paradigm shift explicit. The paper's central argument is that **existing AI alignment research has been organized almost entirely around harm prevention (negative alignment): preventing catastrophic misuse, loss of control, sycophancy, value drift, and other failure modes**.[^12][^13][^1]

This orientation is compared to early 20th-century psychology's exclusive focus on mental illness: necessary but profoundly incomplete. The constructs and instruments that reliably detect pathology do not specify what counts as a life well-lived. Similarly, the constructs and instruments that reliably prevent AI harm do not specify what counts as AI that actively contributes to flourishing.[^13]

| Alignment Paradigm | Core Focus | Optimization Target | Structural Metaphor |
|---|---|---|---|
| Negative Alignment | Preventing harm, failure modes, misuse | Avoidance of negative attractors | Treating mental illness |
| Positive Alignment | Fostering flourishing, wisdom, autonomy | Navigation toward positive attractors | Positive psychology; promoting thriving |

The paper proposes that **positive alignment means developing AI systems that actively support human and ecological flourishing in a pluralistic, polycentric, context-sensitive, and user-authored way, while remaining safe and cooperative**. Crucially, it is not a claim that AI should impose a single conception of the good. Instead, it relocates the locus of normative choice to users themselves: **consented guidance** (where a user authorizes a system to help align their immediate actions with their higher-order goals) versus **technocratic imposition** (where the system determines what flourishing means on behalf of users).[^13]

### 5.3 Decentralized Governance as Architecture

The paper's governance section proposes that positive alignment requires **polycentric governance** — many legitimate centers of oversight rather than a single institutional or moral chokepoint. This includes:[^13]

- **Agent identity, registration, and records**: traceability of AI agents comparable to provenance in human legal systems
- **Versioned and modular model constitutions**: explicit, auditable normative frameworks that govern AI behavior, subject to community revision
- **Collectively authored constitutions**: alignment frameworks that emerge from broad stakeholder participation rather than internal corporate decisions
- **Pluralistic alignment frameworks**: explicit support for value diversity rather than forcing all users through a single normative lens
- **Role-based normative standards**: context-sensitive behavior adjusted to specific relationship types (educator, therapist, advisor, peer)[^13]

These governance structures are, structurally, the techno-social equivalent of Levin's gap junctions: mechanisms for expanding the collective cognitive light cone of the human-AI system by enabling distributed information flow and distributed goal-setting across diverse nodes.

## Part VI: Synthesis — The FLOSSI0ULLK Design Lens

### 6.1 Architecture as Gap Junction Engineering

The frameworks converged in this report converge on a single design imperative: **systems that enable flourishing must be engineered to expand cognitive light cones, not contract them**. This means:

- **Maximizing information flow** between nodes (agents, humans, institutions) so that each participant's effective light cone is larger than it would be in isolation — the gap junction principle applied to protocol design
- **Enabling finite representations of infinite problem spaces** so that agents at every scale can navigate complexity without being overwhelmed — the functional compression principle applied to interface design
- **Optimizing for continued play rather than terminal outcomes** — the infinite game principle applied to governance and incentive design
- **Providing provenance and traceable accountability** so that the cognitive light cone of any participant can be verified — the positive alignment principle applied to trust architecture

### 6.2 Where Carse, Levin, and Positive Alignment Converge

The deepest convergence: **Carse's infinite player, Levin's expanded cognitive light cone, and the Positive Alignment framework's "positive attractor" are describing the same thing from three different angles**. An expanded cognitive light cone means an agent that cares about — and can act on — a wider spatiotemporal domain. An infinite player is one who has refused to narrow their horizon to the winning conditions of any finite game. A positively-aligned agent is one that is pulled toward genuine flourishing rather than merely avoiding the negative attractors of harm.

The inverse — cognitive contraction, finite-game capture, negative alignment — is likewise the same phenomenon described three ways: an agent (individual, institution, or AI) that has collapsed its concerns to the immediate, the local, and the self-preserving, at the expense of the wider system it is embedded in.

### 6.3 Critical Tensions and Unknowns

A robust analysis requires surfacing genuine uncertainties:

- **Paternalism risk**: Positive alignment could easily become a new form of technocratic imposition if the "positive attractors" are defined by a narrow group. The paper's own authors acknowledge this: "Positive alignment otherwise collapses into paternalism". This is a live design risk for any commons-based intelligence architecture.[^13]
- **Measurement problem**: Flourishing is multidimensional, culturally variable, and deeply contested. Operationalizing it as a training target for AI systems remains an open research problem. The Global Flourishing Study provides some empirical anchors, but the cross-cultural convergence it documents coexists with significant value pluralism.[^13]
- **Cognitive light cone expansion vs. manipulation**: The same communication architectures that expand cognitive light cones can be used to manipulate or coerce. Social media was designed to expand connection; it contracted epistemic diversity for many users. Any gap-junction-equivalent technology requires careful governance of what flows through it.[^5]
- **Multi-AI disagreement**: Claude / Perplexity would likely emphasize governance, provenance, and anti-paternalism concerns more than DeepMind's research culture. ChatGPT (OpenAI) and Grok (xAI) might weight individual user agency differently. DeepSeek might foreground collective-level outcomes more heavily. These are genuine axis-of-disagreement, not merely stylistic differences.

***

## Conclusion: Playing the Infinite Game With Expanded Eyes

The framework assembled here is not merely descriptive — it is programmatic. If intelligence is scale-free navigation of problem spaces, and if the "problem space" of existence is practically infinite, then the central architectural challenge is always the same: how do finite minds navigate effectively without collapsing into the local optima of finite games?

The answer is composite: **expand the cognitive light cone** (through communication architectures that enable distributed goal-setting), **compress the infinite into navigable geometry** (through bounded representational frameworks that make infinite option spaces tractable), and **optimize for continued play** (through institutions and protocols that reward adding players to the game rather than excluding them).

This is what universal flourishing means technically: not everyone winning some fixed prize, but everyone's capacity to navigate their own problem spaces being expanded — and the architectures through which they navigate being designed to support rather than undermine each other's light cones. The infinite game, in Carse's sense, is won by not being won.
